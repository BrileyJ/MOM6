2265 !> \namespace mom_hor_visc
2266 !!
2267 !! This module contains the subroutine horizontal_viscosity() that calculates the
2268 !! effects of horizontal viscosity, including parameterizations of the value of
2269 !! the viscosity itself. horizontal_viscosity() calculates the acceleration due to
2270 !! some combination of a biharmonic viscosity and a Laplacian viscosity. Either or
2271 !! both may use a coefficient that depends on the shear and strain of the flow.
2272 !! All metric terms are retained. The Laplacian is calculated as the divergence of
2273 !! a stress tensor, using the form suggested by Smagorinsky (1993). The biharmonic
2274 !! is calculated by twice applying the divergence of the stress tensor that is
2275 !! used to calculate the Laplacian, but without the dependence on thickness in the
2276 !! first pass. This form permits a variable viscosity, and indicates no
2277 !! acceleration for either resting fluid or solid body rotation.
2278 !!
2279 !! The form of the viscous accelerations is discussed extensively in Griffies and
2280 !! Hallberg (2000), and the implementation here follows that discussion closely.
2281 !! We use the notation of Smith and McWilliams (2003) with the exception that the
2282 !! isotropic viscosity is \f$\kappa_h\f$.
2283 !!
2284 !! \section section_horizontal_viscosity Horizontal viscosity in MOM
2285 !!
2286 !! In general, the horizontal stress tensor can be written as
2287 !! \f[
2288 !! {\bf \sigma} =
2289 !! \begin{pmatrix}
2290 !! \frac{1}{2} \left( \sigma_D + \sigma_T \right) & \frac{1}{2} \sigma_S \\\\
2291 !! \frac{1}{2} \sigma_S & \frac{1}{2} \left( \sigma_D - \sigma_T \right)
2292 !! \end{pmatrix}
2293 !! \f]
2294 !! where \f$\sigma_D\f$, \f$\sigma_T\f$ and \f$\sigma_S\f$ are stresses associated with
2295 !! invariant factors in the strain-rate tensor. For a Newtonian fluid, the stress
2296 !! tensor is usually linearly related to the strain-rate tensor. The horizontal
2297 !! strain-rate tensor is
2298 !! \f[
2299 !! \dot{\bf e} =
2300 !! \begin{pmatrix}
2301 !! \frac{1}{2} \left( \dot{e}_D + \dot{e}_T \right) & \frac{1}{2} \dot{e}_S \\\\
2302 !! \frac{1}{2} \dot{e}_S & \frac{1}{2} \left( \dot{e}_D - \dot{e}_T \right)
2303 !! \end{pmatrix}
2304 !! \f]
2305 !! where \f$\dot{e}_D = \partial_x u + \partial_y v\f$ is the horizontal divergence,
2306 !! \f$\dot{e}_T = \partial_x u - \partial_y v\f$ is the horizontal tension, and
2307 !! \f$\dot{e}_S = \partial_y u + \partial_x v\f$ is the horizontal shear strain.
2308 !!
2309 !! The trace of the stress tensor, \f$tr(\bf \sigma) = \sigma_D\f$, is usually
2310 !! absorbed into the pressure and only the deviatoric stress tensor considered.
2311 !! From here on, we drop \f$\sigma_D\f$. The trace of the strain tensor, \f$tr(\bf e) =
2312 !! \dot{e}_D\f$ is non-zero for horizontally divergent flow but only enters the
2313 !! stress tensor through \f$\sigma_D\f$ and so we will drop \f$\sigma_D\f$ from
2314 !! calculations of the strain tensor in the code. Therefore the horizontal stress
2315 !! tensor can be considered to be
2316 !! \f[
2317 !! {\bf \sigma} =
2318 !! \begin{pmatrix}
2319 !! \frac{1}{2} \sigma_T & \frac{1}{2} \sigma_S \\\\
2320 !! \frac{1}{2} \sigma_S & - \frac{1}{2} \sigma_T
2321 !! \end{pmatrix}
2322 !! .\f]
2323 !!
2324 !! The stresses above are linearly related to the strain through a viscosity
2325 !! coefficient, \f$\kappa_h\f$:
2326 !! \f{eqnarray*}{
2327 !! \sigma_T & = & 2 \kappa_h \dot{e}_T \\\\
2328 !! \sigma_S & = & 2 \kappa_h \dot{e}_S
2329 !! .
2330 !! \f}
2331 !!
2332 !! The viscosity \f$\kappa_h\f$ may either be a constant or variable. For example,
2333 !! \f$\kappa_h\f$ may vary with the shear, as proposed by Smagorinsky (1993).
2334 !!
2335 !! The accelerations resulting form the divergence of the stress tensor are
2336 !! \f{eqnarray*}{
2337 !! \hat{\bf x} \cdot \left( \nabla \cdot {\bf \sigma} \right)
2338 !! & = &
2339 !! \partial_x \left( \frac{1}{2} \sigma_T \right)
2340 !! + \partial_y \left( \frac{1}{2} \sigma_S \right)
2341 !! \\\\
2342 !! & = &
2343 !! \partial_x \left( \kappa_h \dot{e}_T \right)
2344 !! + \partial_y \left( \kappa_h \dot{e}_S \right)
2345 !! \\\\
2346 !! \hat{\bf y} \cdot \left( \nabla \cdot {\bf \sigma} \right)
2347 !! & = &
2348 !! \partial_x \left( \frac{1}{2} \sigma_S \right)
2349 !! + \partial_y \left( \frac{1}{2} \sigma_T \right)
2350 !! \\\\
2351 !! & = &
2352 !! \partial_x \left( \kappa_h \dot{e}_S \right)
2353 !! + \partial_y \left( - \kappa_h \dot{e}_T \right)
2354 !! .
2355 !! \f}
2356 !!
2357 !! The form of the Laplacian viscosity in general coordinates is:
2358 !! \f{eqnarray*}{
2359 !! \hat{\bf x} \cdot \left( \nabla \cdot \sigma \right)
2360 !! & = &
2361 !! \frac{1}{h} \left[ \partial_x \left( \kappa_h h \dot{e}_T \right)
2362 !! + \partial_y \left( \kappa_h h \dot{e}_S \right) \right]
2363 !! \\\\
2364 !! \hat{\bf y} \cdot \left( \nabla \cdot \sigma \right)
2365 !! & = &
2366 !! \frac{1}{h} \left[ \partial_x \left( \kappa_h h \dot{e}_S \right)
2367 !! - \partial_y \left( \kappa_h h \dot{e}_T \right) \right]
2368 !! .
2369 !! \f}
2370 !!
2371 !! \subsection section_laplacian_viscosity_coefficient Laplacian viscosity coefficient
2372 !!
2373 !! The horizontal viscosity coefficient, \f$\kappa_h\f$, can have multiple components.
2374 !! The isotropic components are:
2375 !! - A uniform background component, \f$\kappa_{bg}\f$.
2376 !! - A constant but spatially variable 2D map, \f$\kappa_{2d}(x,y)\f$.
2377 !! - A ''MICOM'' viscosity, \f$U_\nu \Delta(x,y)\f$, which uses a constant
2378 !! velocity scale, \f$U_\nu\f$ and a measure of the grid-spacing \f$\Delta(x,y)^2 =
2379 !! \frac{2 \Delta x^2 \Delta y^2}{\Delta x^2 + \Delta y^2}\f$.
2380 !! - A function of
2381 !! latitude, \f$\kappa_{\phi}(x,y) = \kappa_{\pi/2} |\sin(\phi)|^n\f$.
2382 !! - A dynamic Smagorinsky viscosity, \f$\kappa_{Sm}(x,y,t) = C_{Sm} \Delta^2 \sqrt{\dot{e}_T^2 + \dot{e}_S^2}\f$.
2383 !! - A dynamic Leith viscosity, \f$\kappa_{Lth}(x,y,t) =
2384 !! C_{Lth} \Delta^3 \sqrt{|\nabla \zeta|^2 + |\nabla \dot{e}_D|^2}\f$.
2385 !!
2386 !! A maximum stable viscosity, \f$\kappa_{max}(x,y)\f$ is calculated based on the
2387 !! grid-spacing and time-step and used to clip calculated viscosities.
2388 !!
2389 !! The static components of \f$\kappa_h\f$ are first combined as follows:
2390 !! \f[
2391 !! \kappa_{static} = \min \left[ \max\left(
2392 !! \kappa_{bg},
2393 !! U_\nu \Delta(x,y),
2394 !! \kappa_{2d}(x,y),
2395 !! \kappa_\phi(x,y)
2396 !! \right)
2397 !! , \kappa_{max}(x,y) \right]
2398 !! \f]
2399 !! and stored in the module control structure as variables <code>Kh_bg_xx</code> and
2400 !! <code>Kh_bg_xy</code> for the tension (h-points) and shear (q-points) components
2401 !! respectively.
2402 !!
2403 !! The full viscosity includes the dynamic components as follows:
2404 !! \f[
2405 !! \kappa_h(x,y,t) = r(\Delta,L_d)
2406 !! \max \left( \kappa_{static}, \kappa_{Sm}, \kappa_{Lth} \right)
2407 !! \f]
2408 !! where \f$r(\Delta,L_d)\f$ is a resolution function.
2409 !!
2410 !! The dynamic Smagorinsky and Leith viscosity schemes are exclusive with each
2411 !! other.
2412 !!
2413 !! \subsection section_viscous_boundary_conditions Viscous boundary conditions
2414 !!
2415 !! Free slip boundary conditions have been coded, although no slip boundary
2416 !! conditions can be used with the Laplacian viscosity based on the 2D land-sea
2417 !! mask. For a western boundary, for example, the boundary conditions with the
2418 !! biharmonic operator would be written as:
2419 !! \f[
2420 !! \partial_x v = 0 ; \partial_x^3 v = 0 ; u = 0 ; \partial_x^2 u = 0 ,
2421 !! \f]
2422 !! while for a Laplacian operator, they are simply
2423 !! \f[
2424 !! \partial_x v = 0 ; u = 0 .
2425 !! \f]
2426 !! These boundary conditions are largely dictated by the use of an Arakawa
2427 !! C-grid and by the varying layer thickness.
2428 !!
2429 !! \subsection section_anisotropic_viscosity Anisotropic viscosity
2430 !!
2431 !! Large et al., 2001, proposed enhancing viscosity in a particular direction and the
2432 !! approach was generalized in Smith and McWilliams, 2003. We use the second form of their
2433 !! two coefficient anisotropic viscosity (section 4.3). We also replace their
2434 !! \f$A^\prime\f$ and $D$ such that \f$2A^\prime = 2 \kappa_h + D\f$ and
2435 !! \f$\kappa_a = D\f$ so that \f$\kappa_h\f$ can be considered the isotropic
2436 !! viscosity and \f$\kappa_a=D\f$ can be consider the anisotropic viscosity. The
2437 !! direction of anisotropy is defined by a unit vector \f$\hat{\bf
2438 !! n}=(n_1,n_2)\f$.
2439 !!
2440 !! The contributions to the stress tensor are
2441 !! \f[
2442 !! \begin{pmatrix}
2443 !! \sigma_T \\\\ \sigma_S
2444 !! \end{pmatrix}
2445 !! =
2446 !! \left[
2447 !! \begin{pmatrix}
2448 !! 2 \kappa_h + \kappa_a & 0 \\\\
2449 !! 0 & 2 \kappa_h
2450 !! \end{pmatrix}
2451 !! + 2 \kappa_a n_1 n_2
2452 !! \begin{pmatrix}
2453 !! - 2 n_1 n_2 & n_1^2 - n_2^2 \\\\
2454 !! n_1^2 - n_2^2 & 2 n_1 n_2
2455 !! \end{pmatrix}
2456 !! \right]
2457 !! \begin{pmatrix}
2458 !! \dot{e}_T \\\\ \dot{e}_S
2459 !! \end{pmatrix}
2460 !! \f]
2461 !! Dissipation of kinetic energy requires \f$\kappa_h \geq 0\f$ and \f$2 \kappa_h + \kappa_a \geq 0\f$.
2462 !! Note that when anisotropy is aligned with the x-direction, \f$n_1 = \pm 1\f$, then
2463 !! \f$n_2 = 0\f$ and the cross terms vanish. The accelerations in this aligned limit
2464 !! with constant coefficients become
2465 !! \f{eqnarray*}{
2466 !! \hat{\bf x} \cdot \nabla \cdot {\bf \sigma}
2467 !! & = &
2468 !! \partial_x \left( \left( \kappa_h + \frac{1}{2} \kappa_a \right) \dot{e}_T \right)
2469 !! + \partial_y \left( \kappa_h \dot{e}_S \right)
2470 !! \\\\
2471 !! & = &
2472 !! \left( \kappa_h + \kappa_a \right) \partial_{xx} u
2473 !! + \kappa_h \partial_{yy} u
2474 !! - \frac{1}{2} \kappa_a \partial_x \left( \partial_x u + \partial_y v \right)
2475 !! \\\\
2476 !! \hat{\bf y} \cdot \nabla \cdot {\bf \sigma}
2477 !! & = &
2478 !! \partial_x \left( \kappa_h \dot{e}_S \right)
2479 !! - \partial_y \left( \left( \kappa_h + \frac{1}{2} \kappa_a \right) \dot{e}_T \right)
2480 !! \\\\
2481 !! & = &
2482 !! \kappa_h \partial_{xx} v
2483 !! + \left( \kappa_h + \kappa_a \right) \partial_{yy} v
2484 !! - \frac{1}{2} \kappa_a \partial_y \left( \partial_x u + \partial_y v \right)
2485 !! \f}
2486 !! which has contributions akin to a negative divergence damping (a divergence
2487 !! enhancement?) but which is weaker than the enhanced tension terms by half.
2488 !!
2489 !! \subsection section_viscous_discretization Discretization
2490 !!
2491 !! The horizontal tension, \f$\dot{e}_T\f$, is stored in variable <code>sh_xx</code> and
2492 !! discretized as
2493 !! \f[
2494 !! \dot{e}_T
2495 !! = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} \delta_i \left( \frac{1}{\Delta y} u \right)
2496 !! - \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta y} \delta_j \left( \frac{1}{\Delta x} v \right)
2497 !! .
2498 !! \f]
2499 !! The horizontal divergent strain, \f$\dot{e}_D\f$, is stored in variable
2500 !! <code>div_xx</code> and discretized as
2501 !! \f[
2502 !! \dot{e}_D
2503 !! = \frac{1}{h A} \left( \delta_i \left( \overline{h}^i \Delta y \, u \right)

2505 !! .
2506 !! \f]

2508 !! continuity equation.
2509 !!
2510 !! The horizontal shear strain, \f$\dot{e}_S\f$, is stored in variable <code>sh_xy</code>
2511 !! and discretized as
2512 !! \f[
2513 !! \dot{e}_S = v_x + u_y
2514 !! \f]
2515 !! where
2516 !! \f{align*}{
2517 !! v_x &= \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} \delta_i \left( \frac{1}{\Delta y} v \right) \\\\
2518 !! u_y &= \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta y} \delta_j \left( \frac{1}{\Delta x} u \right)
2519 !! \f}
2520 !! which are calculated separately so that no-slip or free-slip boundary
2521 !! conditions can be applied to \f$v_x\f$ and \f$u_y\f$ where appropriate.
2522 !!
2523 !! The tendency for the x-component of the divergence of stress is stored in
2524 !! variable <code>diffu</code> and discretized as
2525 !! \f[
2526 !! \hat{\bf x} \cdot \left( \nabla \cdot {\bf \sigma} \right) =
2527 !! \frac{1}{A \overline{h}^i} \left(
2528 !! \frac{1}{\Delta y} \delta_i \left( h \Delta y^2 \kappa_h \dot{e}_T \right) +
2529 !! \frac{1}{\Delta x} \delta_j \left( \tilde{h}^{ij} \Delta x^2 \kappa_h \dot{e}_S \right)
2530 !! \right)
2531 !! .
2532 !! \f]
2533 !!
2534 !! The tendency for the y-component of the divergence of stress is stored in
2535 !! variable <code>diffv</code> and discretized as
2536 !! \f[
2537 !! \hat{\bf y} \cdot \left( \nabla \cdot {\bf \sigma} \right) =
2538 !! \frac{1}{A \overline{h}^j} \left(
2539 !! \frac{1}{\Delta y} \delta_i \left( \tilde{h}^{ij} \Delta y^2 A_M \dot{e}_S \right)
2540 !! - \frac{1}{\Delta x} \delta_j \left( h \Delta x^2 A_M \dot{e}_T \right)
2541 !! \right)
2542 !! .
